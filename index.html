I've been taking things apart since before I understood what was inside them. Prying open computers to see how they worked turned into building them, and then into modeling the parts I couldn't buy. Somewhere between a childhood spent inside a desktop tower and a CAD window open on a second monitor, mechanical engineering stopped being a subject and became the way I think — designing hardware that has to physically fit, power on, and survive being used, where a tenth of a millimeter decides whether a part seats or goes back on the print bed.

About

DRAWN BY
C. STENSRUD

I'm a mechanical engineering student who never really left the workbench. The habit started early — computers were the first machines I was allowed to break, and CAD was how I stopped needing to buy the parts I wanted.

Most of what I build lives at the intersection of hardware and software: repurposing decommissioned machines, designing enclosures and adapters that have to hold real tolerances, and printing the pieces that make a hack look like a product. I'm comfortable moving from a caliper reading to an Inventor model to a finished print, and just as comfortable inside a Linux terminal or a laptop's board-level repair.

I care about the details that don't show up in a render — the fitment, the thermals, the one dimension that decides whether a design is finished or just close.
